Sturm-Liouville and Orthogonal Functions

These are notes for a discussion about linear algebra and analysis for the students of the Research Club.

1 Linear algebra

Let \(V\) be an \(𝐑\)-vector space, and recall that a linear operator \(L\) on \(V\) is a linear map \[L:V ⟶ V\] An eigenvector of \(L\) is a vector \(v_λ ∈ V\) such that \[L v_λ = λ v_λ\] The number \(λ ∈ V\) is called the eigenvalue of \(L\) associated to the eigenvector \(v_λ\).

Suppose \(V\) is equipped with an inner product \[\left< ⋅ , ⋅ \right> : V×V ⟶ 𝐑\] The adjoint \(L^\dagger\) of \(L\) with respect to the inner product is a linear map \[L^\dagger :V ⟶ V\] defined by the formula \[\left< v , L^\dagger u \right> = \left< L v , u \right> \quad \forall \quad u,v ∈ V\]

Exercise 1. Let \(V = 𝐑^n\) and take the inner product to be the Euclidean one. Let \(L\) be a matrix and find the entries of \(L^\dagger\) in terms of those of \(L\).

Since \(L^\dagger\) and \(L\) are both operators on \(V\), we can compare then. An operator is called self-adjoint if \(L^\dagger = L\).

Exercise 2. Let \(v_λ\) and \(v_μ\) be eigenvectors of \(L\) and suppose \(L\) is self-adjoint. Compare the “matrix elements” \(\left< v_μ, L v_λ\right>\) and \(\left< v_μ, L^\dagger v_λ\right>\) to conclude that \(v_λ\) is orthogonal to \(v_μ\) (with respect to the inner product) if \(μ ≠ λ\).

Consider the special case in which \(\mathrm dim\ V = 2\) with the Euclidean inner product, and let \(v₁ , v₂ ∈ V\) be linearly-independent vectors. Compute the Euclidean length \(|v₁|\), and define the unit vector \[e₁ = { v₁ \over |v₁| }\] We will now make a new vector \(ṽ₂\) defined by \[ṽ₂ := v₂ - \left< v₂ , e₁ \right> e₁\] It is orthogonal to \(e₁\) since \(e₁\) is a unit vector. Compute the length \(|ṽ₂|\) and define the unit vector \[e₂ = { ṽ₂ \over |ṽ₂| }\] By construction \[\left< e_i , e_j \right> = δ_{ij}\] so \(V=\mathrm{Span}\{ e_i\}_{i=1}²\) is spanned by these “ortho(gonal)-normal(ized)” vectors.

Exercise 3 (Gram-Schmidt). Use mathematical induction to extend this result to a procedure applicable to any finite-dimensional inner-product space. This algorithm is called the Gram-Schmidt orthonormalization process.

Together with the result on orthogonal eigenvectors for self-adjoint operators, we conclude that if we have a vector space equipped with such an operator, we can construct an orthonormal basis of eigenvectors for the vector space.

By convention, the Legendre polynomials \(P_n\) are normalized by \[\left< P_m , P_n\right>:= \int_{-1}¹ P_m(x) P_n(x) dx = {2 \over 2n +1} δ_{mn}\] The Rodriguez formula gives them as \[P_n(x) = \frac1{2^nn!} \frac{d^n}{dx^n} (x² - 1)^n\] These polynomials are the eigenvectors of the operator \(L\) of [LegendreLaplacian].

Exercise 6. Check this on the first few Legendre polynomials \(p_i\) for \(i=0, ... , 3\). What are the eigenvalues?

2.1.0.1 Laplace equation

The Legendre polynomials are famous because they are instrumental to the solution of the Laplace equation \(Δ 𝜑 =0\) in three dimensions. In spherical coordinates, the three-dimensional Laplacian operator is \[Δ = \frac {1}{r²} \frac{∂}{∂r} \left( r² \frac{∂}{∂r}\ ⋅ \right) +\frac{1}{r² \sin θ } \frac{∂}{∂θ} \left( \sin θ \frac{∂}{∂θ}\ ⋅ \right) +\frac{1}{r² \sin θ } \frac{∂²}{∂φ²}\] To solve a problem with spherical symmetry, the Ansatz is made that the solution is separable: \(𝜑(r, θ, φ) = R(r) Θ(θ) 𝛷(φ)\). This allows the separation of the Laplace equation into three parts. It can be shown that the \(𝛷=\) constant and that the rest of the equation separates into the sum of two parts with one depending only on \(r\) and the other only on \(θ\). This means that the two must be equal to the same constant. The \(θ\)-dependent part is given by \[\frac{1}{\sin θ } \frac{d}{dθ} \left( \sin θ \frac{d Θ}{dθ} \right) + c Θ = 0.\] (The separation constant is denoted by \(c\).)

Exercise 7. Make the change of variables \(x = \cos θ\) to show that this is an eigenvalue equation for the Legendre operator [LegendreLaplacian].

2.1.0.2 An aside on Taylor series

The completeness of eigenfunctions of a self-adjoint operator on an infinite-dimensional vector space is striking, but it should not be entirely shocking to a student of calculus. Recall that a real function \(f\) of a real variable \(x\) is analytic on a domain \(D⊂ 𝐑\) if there is a convergent series on \(D\) that agrees with the function \(f\) on \(D\): \[f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^∞ a_n x^n \quad \quad \textrm{on}\quad D.\]

Exercise 8. Find an expression for the coefficients \(\{a_n\}_{n=0}^∞\) in terms of \(f\) and its derivatives.

2.2 (Anti-)Periodic Functions

Let \(S=(-π, π)\), and consider the operator \[L = \frac{d²}{dx²}\] on \(L²(S)\). Then \[\int g \frac{d²}{dx²} f dx = \left. g \frac{df}{dx} \right|_{x=-π}^π - \int \frac{dg}{dx}\frac{df}{dx} dx = \left. g \frac{df}{dx} \right|_{x=-π}^π - \left. \frac{dg}{dx} f \right|_{x=-π}^π +\int \left(\frac{d²}{dx²} g\right) f dx\] The boundary terms will vanish if we restrict to the subset of \(L²(S)\) of functions that vanish at \(x =± π\) or have vanishing first derivatives at those points. Let’s call the former \(𝒟 (S)\) and the latter \(𝒩 (S)\).

Exercise 9. Find the eigenvectors and eigenvalues of the self-adjoint operator \(L\) on \(𝒟(S)\) and \(𝒩(S)\). Show that they are orthogonal and normalize them.

If the eigenvectors \(\{v_n(x)\}_{n∈ 𝐙}\), say of a self-adjoint operator span the vector space on which that operator acts, then in the context of \(L²(S)\), that must mean that it is possible to decompose any function \(f∈ L²(S)\) in a (possibly infinite) sum of these eigenvectors \(f(x) = \sum_{n∈ 𝐙} c_n v_n(x)\). Alternatively, for some series of real coefficients \(a_n, b_n ∈ 𝐑\ \forall n\). This form is called the Fourier series representation of the function \(f(x)\). It gives an entire function in terms of a series \(\{a_n\}_{n=1}^∞\) and \(\{b_n\}_{n=0}^∞\) of real numbers called the Fourier coefficients.

Exercise 10. Assuming this decomposition of the function and using the orthogonality of the eigen-functions you found above, calculate the \(a\) and \(b\) coefficients.
