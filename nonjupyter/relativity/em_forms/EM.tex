\subsection{Integral form}

We are going to describe the Maxwell equations in integral form. For this, we need a bunch of definitions. Let 
\begin{itemize}
\item $U ‚äā ūĚźĎ¬≥$ be a 3-dimensional region in 3-space 
\item $dv$ denote the volume element on $U$
\item $‚ąāU$ denote the (2-dimensional) boundary of $U$

\item $da$ denote the area element on $‚ąāU$ induced from the canonical volume form on $ūĚźĎ¬≥$ and let $d{\bf a} = {\bf n} da$.

The Maxwell equations are 
\begin{description}
\item[Coulomb] Let $Q(U)$ be the charge contained in $U$. Then

%Q(U) = \frac 1{4 ŌÄ } ‚ąę_{‚ąāU} {\bf E} ‚ąô {\bf n}\ da

\subsection{Superior mathematical form}
The differential ‚Äúcalc 3‚ÄĚ notation is clearly superior to the integral form, it is still dirty for various reasons. 

\begin{exercise}
Restrict the entire universe to two dimensions instead of 3. What does the cross product mean in this 2D universe? 
Extend to a 4D universe by adding an additional direction to up/down, left/right, forward/backward. Can you define the cross product there? 
\end{exercise}

The problem is that the existence of the cross product is an accident of 3D. A cross product is an anti-symmetric pairing to two vectors/vector fields to give a third vector (field) and this does not need to exist in arbitrary dimension. 

We will return to the reason it exists in 3D eventually, but for now, let's see how we can salvage the Maxwell equations by stripping off the 3D-ness. To do this, we need some formalism.

\begin{definition}
Let $V$ be a real vector space. A linear form on $V$ is a linear map $V ‚Üí ūĚźĎ$. The space of linear forms on $V$ is denoted by $V*$; it is called the dual of $V$.
\end{definition}

\begin{claim}
Let $\textrm{dim}_ūĚźĎ (V) < ‚ąě$. Then $V*$ is a vector space and $\textrm{dim}_ūĚźĎ (V*) = \textrm{dim}_ūĚźĎ (V)$.
\end{claim}

\begin{definition}
Let $ūĚíĖ$ be a vector field on $U‚äā ūĚźĎ¬≥$ and let $‚Ěīx^i ‚ĚĶ_{i=1}¬≥$ be a set of coordinates on $U$. In these coordinates
$$
ūĚíĖ = u^i(x) ‚ąā_i = u^i(x) \frac ‚ąā{‚ąāx^i}
$$ 
For the functions $u^i :U‚Üí ūĚźĎ$ for $i=1,2,3$ are called the components of $ūĚíĖ$ (in the coordinates $x$).
\end{definition}

Take careful note of the position of the indices in the expressions above: The the component functions of vector fields have their indices ‚Äúup‚ÄĚ, whereas the coordinate basis vectors $‚ąā$ have their indices ‚Äúdown‚ÄĚ.

Now suppose we have the component functions $‚Ěīu^i‚ĚĶ_{i=1}¬≥$ for some vector field $ūĚíĖ$. I'm getting tired of typing that out, so when the meaning is clear, I will abbreviate this as $‚Ěīu^i‚ĚĶ$. 
Anyway, to map from a set like this to a real function, we need to ‚Äúget rid of the index‚ÄĚ: If we have 3 other functions $‚ĚīŌČ_i(x)‚ĚĶ$, we can make 
$$
ŌČ(ūĚíĖ) = ŌČ_i(x) u^i(x)
$$
which is a function $U‚Üí ūĚźĎ$, as we wanted when we defined linear forms. 

Our conclusion is that under the conditions set out in the Claim, the dual space is spanned by elements with coordinate functions $‚ĚīŌČ_i‚ĚĶ$ analogous to the $‚Ěīu^i‚ĚĶ$ of a vector field, but with the indices in the other position. There is a good reason for this, but for the moment you can just take it as a rule. 

If you don't want to hit the ‚ÄúI believe‚ÄĚ  button, consider the following: Let $L : V‚Üí V$ denote a linear operator on a vector space $V$, and consider its action on a vector $ūĚíĖ ‚Ü¶ L(ūĚíĖ)$. This operator does not act on $ūĚźĎ$ so if we consider a linear form $ŌČ : V‚Üí ūĚźĎ$ then it maps $ūĚíĖ ‚Ü¶ ŌČ(ūĚíĖ) ‚ąą ūĚźĎ$. This implies that $L$ must act on $ŌČ$ in a way that ensures that it has no effect on $ŌČ(ūĚíĖ)$ (since it is an element of $ūĚźĎ$ and $L$ has no effect there.

\begin{exercise}\footnote{You will probably not know what to make of this question and how to even start, but that's ok. If you think hard, then when we talk about it, it will make more sense and it will be a good investment.}
By working in components, compare what the matrix of $L$ does to the components of $ūĚíĖ$ and show that that is not the same as what it does to the components of $ŌČ$. Note that it {\em is} related, but not the same.
\end{exercise} 

A linear form $V‚Üí ūĚźĎ$ is also called a 1-form. When we have a coordinate basis for $V$ denoted by $‚Ěī‚ąā_i= ‚ąā/‚ąāx^i‚ĚĶ$ , then there is a natural basis for $V*$ denoted $‚Ěīdx^i‚ĚĶ$. (Note the position of the index.) Since 1-forms map vectors to numbers, it must be true that the $dx$s map the $‚ąā$s to numbers. The definition/normalization for Cartesian coordinates is this:\footnote{The symbol on the RHS is called the ‚ÄúKronecker delta‚ÄĚ symbol/tensor.}
$$
dx^i ( ‚ąā_j ) = őī^i_j = \begin{cases} 
	1 & \textrm{if } i=j\\
	0 & \textrm{otherwise}
	\end{cases}
$$

\begin{exercise}
We can combine all theses things into the following synthesis: Let $ūĚíĖ$ be a vector field and let $ŌČ$ be a 1-form field, both defined on $U‚äā ūĚźĎ^n$ for general $n$. Let $‚Ěīx^i‚ĚĶ$ denote coordinates on $U$. In these coordinates, $ūĚíĖ=u^i(x) ‚ąā_i$ and $ŌČ = ŌČ_i(x) dx^i$ where the repeated indices are summed (as always). $$

$$
\end{exercise}
This is an example of a calculation done in a local coordinates called a ‚Äúcomponent calculation‚ÄĚ. We will do many of these. 

\paragraph{Multilinear algebra}
We can make combinations of the basis 1-forms by taking more copies of $V*$ in what is called the ‚Äútensor product‚ÄĚ. Operationally, the tensor product $V*‚äóV*$ is just the collection of objects with basis $dx^i ‚äó dx^j$ where the $‚äó$ symbol roughly means ‚Äúkeep them in this order‚ÄĚ. For example, if $M ‚ąą V*‚äóV*$ then what this means is that in local coordinates $M = M_{ij} dx^i ‚äó dx^j$. We recognize the components $M_{ij}$ as those of an $n√ó n$ matrix. 

We can similarly do this for $V$ as well and we can mix $V$ and $V*$. Furthermore, we can make arbitrarily higher numbers of factors like $V ‚äó V ‚äó V* ‚äó V* ‚äó V$. Often, we rearrange the factors so that all the vector spaces come first and then all the duals,\footnote{It is important to note that we are keeping the order in each type of factor, but we are listing all the duals (in their original order) ‚Äúat the back‚ÄĚ.} so we could write this as $V ‚äó V ‚äó V ‚äó V* ‚äó V*$. 

\begin{exercise}
Show that 
\begin{enumerate}
\item linear operators are elements of $V ‚äó V*$
\item inner products are elements of $V* ‚äó V*$
\item the 3D cross product is an element $V ‚äó V* ‚äó V*$
\end{enumerate}
Hint: What do you need to provide to each of these to get a scalar?
\end{exercise}

An element of $V ‚äó \cdots ‚äó V ‚äó V* ‚äó \cdots ‚äó V*$ with $q$ factors of $V$ and $p$ factors of $V*$ is called a {\em tensor} of rank $(q,p)$. Tensors of low rank often have special names: A tensor of rank
\begin{itemize}
\item $(0,0)$ is a scalar
\item $(1,0)$ is a vector
\item $(0,1)$ is a linear form or 1-form
\item $(0,2)$ is a bilinear form 2-form
\item $(0,q)$ is a multi-linear form of rank $q$ or $q$-form
\item $(1,1)$ is a linear operator
\item $(q,p)$ with $p+q=2$ are also called matrices
\end{itemize}

Permutations have a natural action on tensors, and we use them to reduce the general tensors to simpler elements. For example a $(0,2)$ tensor $T$ can be split into symmetric $S$ and anti-symmetric $A$ parts: $T_{ij} = S_{ij} + A_{ij}$. This is so useful that we use a special notation on the indices:
$$
S_{ij} = T_{(ij)} = \frac12 (T_{ij} + T_{ji})
\quad \quad \textrm{and}\quad \quad 
A_{ij} = T_{[ij]} = \frac12 (T_{ij} - T_{ji})
$$

The anti-symmetric part of $V* ‚äó V*$ is so nice that it gets a special name: $V*\wedge V*$. The reason it is nice is related to the following 
\begin{exercise}
Let $f:U‚Üí ūĚźĎ$ be a smooth function and define the Hessian matrix of $f$ by $H(f)$ as the matrix of second partial derivatives of $f$. Show that $H(f)$ is in the kernel of the map $V*‚äó V*‚Üí V*\wedge V*$. 
\end{exercise} 
An exterior $q$-form is an element 
$$
ŌČ ‚ąą őõ^q = ‚čÄ_{i=1}^q V*= V*\wedge \cdots \wedge  V*
$$
In local coordinates $‚Ěīx^i‚ĚĶ_{i=1}^n$ on $U$, an exterior form of rank $p$ can be written as 
$$
ŌČ = ŌČ_{i‚āĀ ... i_p}(x) dx^{i‚āĀ} \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_p}
%\quad \quad \textrm{where} \quad \quad 
%ŌČ_{i‚āĀ ... i_p}(x)
$$
%(As always, each $i_j = 1,..., \mathrm{dim}(U)$.)
where the component functions $ŌČ_{i‚āĀ ... i_p}(x) = ŌČ_{[i‚āĀ ... i_p]}(x)$ are totally anti-symmetric under permutation of indices.
\begin{exercise}

\begin{enumerate}
	\item $\mathrm{dim}(őõ^p) = {n \choose p}$, in particular $őõ^p = ‚ąÖ$ if $p>n$. 
	\item $őõ^p ‚čÄ őõ^q ‚ČÖ őõ^{p+q}$
	\item for $ŌČ ‚ąą őõ^p$ and $ő∑ ‚ąą őõ^q$, $ŌČ \wedge ő∑ = (-1)^{pq} ő∑ \wedge ŌČ$, in particular $ŌČ¬≤ = 0$ if $p$ is odd.

Together with the fact that a $p$-form is still a $p$-form if you multiply by a real scalar, this exercise shows that the space of exterior forms is an algebra; we call it $őõ^‚ąô$. 
\begin{definition}
Let $d_i : őõ^i ‚Üí őõ^{i+1}$ for $i=0,\dots,n$ be a set of maps. 
Such a set of maps is called a differential on $U$ if $d_{i+1} ‚ąė d_i = 0$ for all $i$. 
We define an extension of these maps to forms of any degree by defining $d_i : őõ^j ‚Üí őõ^{j+1}$ to be the 0 map whenever $i‚Č† j$. Finally, using this extension, we define $d : őõ^p(U) ‚Üí őõ^{p+1}(U)$ for any $p$ by $d = ‚ąĎ_{i=0}^n d_i$. 
\end{definition}
Here is an important application/example: For each $i,p=0,...,n$, let $ŌČ‚ąą őõ^p(U)$ and define 
$$

	‚ąā_k ŌČ_{j‚āĀ ... j_i}(x) dx^k \wedge dx^{j‚āĀ} \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{j_i} & \textrm{if } i=p\\
	0 & \textrm{otherwise} 
	\end{cases}
$$

	\item Show that, for any $p$, $d¬≤ = 0$ as a map $őõ^p‚Üí őõ^{p+2}$.

This exercise has very interesting consequences for mathematics: An algebra of exterior  forms equipped with a differential is called a differential algebra, so the first two conditions are saying that there is such a thing embedded in the structure of calculus. The third part of the exercise is interesting, because you cannot drop the qualifier that $U$ is an open ball.  

A form $ŌČ ‚ąą őõ^p(U)$ is called ‚Äúclosed‚ÄĚ if $dŌČ=0$. A form $ŌČ ‚ąą őõ^p(U)$ that can be written as $ŌČ = d őĽ$ for some $(p-1)$-form $őĽ$ is called ‚Äúexact‚ÄĚ. However, closed forms are not always exact.\footnote{For example, if $U‚ČÖ S¬Ļ$ is a circle and $ŌČ‚ąą őõ¬Ļ(S¬Ļ)$ is a 1-form, then it is automatically closed because $dŌČ$ has degree 2, but that must vanish in a space of dimension 1. But there is no function $f:S¬Ļ ‚Üí ūĚźĎ$ defined uniquely on every point of the circle for which $df$ is the same form as $ŌČ$.}

Often in physics, quantities that we refer to as ‚Äúvectors‚ÄĚ are actually 1-forms. For example, while it is true that the gradient $‚ąáf$ of a function is a vector, the more natural object is actually the 1-form $df$. The two are related with the dot product: A dot product $\langle ‚ąô , ‚ąô \rangle$ is a map $V‚äóV‚Üí ūĚźĎ$ taking two vectors $(ūĚíĖ, ūĚíó )‚Ü¶ \langle ūĚíĖ, ūĚíó \rangle = ūĚíĖ ‚ąô ūĚíó $. This means that if we fill only the first slot, say, with a vector $\langle ūĚíĖ , ‚ąô \rangle$ then we get a map $V‚Üí ūĚźĎ$, that is, we get an element of $V*$, or in yet other words, we get a 1-form. You should confirm that 
$$
df = \langle ‚ąá f, \cdot \rangle 
$$
In fact, if you think about how we make a gradient, we take the components of $(df)_i = ‚ąā_i f$ and arrange them as a column vector: 
$$
( (df)_x , (df)_y, (df)_z) ) = (‚ąāf/‚ąāx, ‚ąāf/‚ąāy, ‚ąāf/‚ąāz) ‚ü∂ 
\ma{‚ąāf/‚ąāx\\ ‚ąāf/‚ąāy\\ ‚ąāf/‚ąāz}
$$
But that means that we are making a vector out of the components of the 1-form!\footnote{In Euclidean space the difference is minor, because the components of the inner product in a cartesian basis are either $0$ or $1$ and this allows us to identify $‚ąā¬Ļf = ‚ąā‚āĀf$, {\it et cetera}. However in special relativity, we add time as a coordinate and the inner product for this part is $-1$ instead of $1$. In that case it really does matter whether we mean $x^m$ or $x_m$ for $m=0,1,2,3$. In geometry or in general relativity the inner product is a complicated collection of functions. Sometimes, there is not even an inner product, so you cannot define the gradient at all! }

\subsection{Integration}
Differential forms can be integrated. To integrate a $1-form$, we need a 1-dimensional subspace of $ūĚźĎ^n$, that is, a curve. To integrate a $2-form$, we need a surface, and we integrate a 3-form over a volume, {\it et cetera}.
